Endpoint governance for agentic AI tools.

The Problem

AI coding tools now execute commands, access networks, and modify files on developer endpoints. Security teams often lack consistent visibility into which tools are active, what they are doing, and whether use aligns with policy.

Name-based blocklists are brittle when tools can be renamed, wrapped, or run as local components.

What Detec Finds

Detec detects by capability and behavior, not product naming, so new tools map to policy classes automatically.

How Detec Decides

Every decision is based on five correlated signal layers: Process, File, Network, Identity, and Behavior.

Signals feed an attribution engine that produces an explainable confidence score from 0.00 to 1.00, then applies policy state.

Enforcement Ladder

Detect
Visibility only.
Warn
Operator notified.
Approval Required
Authorized sign-off required.
Block
Hard stop for policy violations.
  • Low-risk tools can stay at visibility.
  • Medium-risk activity triggers warnings.
  • High-risk actions can require approval.
  • Disallowed behavior can be blocked with evidence.

Audit and Evidence

  • What was detected: tool, class, version.
  • How detection occurred: matching signal layers.
  • Confidence score with contributing weights.
  • Applied policy: enforcement state and rule ID.
  • Resulting action: detect, warn, approval required, or block.

Known Limits

  • Remote/containerized development can reduce host telemetry.
  • Renamed binaries and custom forks require behavior correlation.
  • Short-lived network bursts may need EDR-grade process-to-socket attribution.
  • Evasion is possible; multi-signal correlation is designed to increase evasion cost.
